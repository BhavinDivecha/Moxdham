JAIN


Sallekhana also known as samlehna, santhara, samadhi-marana or sanyasana-marana, is a supplementary vow to the ethical code of conduct of Jainism. It is the religious practice of voluntarily fasting to death by gradually reducing the intake of food and liquids. It is viewed in Jainism as the thinning of human passions and the body, and another means of destroying rebirth-influencing karma by withdrawing all physical and mental activities. It is not considered as a suicide by Jain scholars because it is not an act of passion, nor does it employ poisons or weapons. After the sallekhana vow, the ritual preparation and practice can extend into years.

Jain: Final Rites

Jain cremation ceremony is very simple and short. This is the order of the Rites:

Dressing the body:

  1. Avoid natural flowers
  2. Use sandalwood mala (Garland)
  3. Near the deceased casket
  4. Place swastika
  5. On top of swastika: put a whole coconut, divo, and agarbatti
  6. Recite stavans and bhajans for about one hour
  7. Eulogy from family and friends
  8. Final view by visitors
  9. Family members get together by the casket and apply:
  10. Water and rice three times on the body
  11. Sandalwood powder on the body
  12. Ghee on forehead, hands, and feet.
  13. At the end of the ceremony, guests chant navkar mantra, and put:
  14. Sandalwood mala on body
  15. Piece of gold in the mouth
  16. A pearl in the right eye
  17. The casket is then closed
  • The Jains cremate the dead as soon as possible. First the body is rubbed with a wet cloth. The corpse is then clothed and placed in a bier and covered with a kafan. The body is tied to the bier and taken to the crematorium. A suitable place without any living organisms like grass or insects is selected so as not to harm them. There a platform of wood is erected.
  • The body is taken from the bier and placed on the platform with logs of wood over it. Ghee, camphor and sandalwood powder are sprinkled all over the body and the eldest son of the deceased does the last rites.
  • The son goes round the pyre three times sprinkling water allover the body. Chanting the Namokar Mantra ,he lights up the pyre. Then after sometime they pour milk over the place. The remains are collected in bags and the place is thoroughly cleansed. The remains are not immersed in rivers as they can pollute the water. Instead they dig a hole in the earth and put the remains and sprinkle salt all over, so that it dissolves easily.
  • The Jains believe that the dead soul would be reborn immediately. So for them death is a festival or Mahotsav. Loud wailing and observing anniversaries are not part of the Jain Tradition.
